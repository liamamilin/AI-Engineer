4  Chapter 2 — The AI Engineering Stack

4.1 Introduction

Modern AI systems are not defined by a single component, but by a layered architecture. Just as classical software systems rely on structured stacks—operating systems, runtime environments, application frameworks, and services—AI systems require a structured organization of models, data, control mechanisms, and infrastructure.

Understanding this structure is essential to practicing AI engineering. Without a clear model of the stack, systems quickly become fragile, difficult to maintain, and impossible to reason about. Engineers may experiment with prompts or models in isolation, but without a systematic architecture, such experiments rarely evolve into reliable systems.

The AI engineering stack provides a conceptual framework for organizing the components of intelligent systems. It identifies the major layers through which intelligence is produced, controlled, and deployed. By understanding these layers and their interactions, engineers can design systems that are coherent, maintainable, and scalable.

This chapter introduces the AI engineering stack in detail. We begin by examining the layers individually, then explore how they group into three architectural domains, and finally consider how the stack guides system design and development.

4.2 The Individual Layers

At a high level, the AI engineering stack can be represented as a sequence of interacting layers:

model
data
prompt
tool
agent
pipeline
evaluation
infrastructure

Each layer introduces specific capabilities and assumes particular responsibilities. Let us examine each layer in turn.

4.2.1 Model

The model layer forms the computational foundation of any AI system. It includes the trained machine learning models that perform inference, such as:

  • Language models: Large language models (LLMs) that process and generate natural language
  • Embedding models: Models that transform text or other inputs into vector representations for semantic search and comparison
  • Vision models: Models that process and generate images or analyze visual content
  • Specialized models: Models trained for specific tasks such as translation, summarization, or code generation

These models embody statistical intelligence. They have learned patterns from vast datasets during training and can perform tasks that would be difficult or impossible to specify through explicit algorithms. However, models alone are rarely sufficient for building complete applications. They require direction, context, and integration with other components.

4.2.2 Data

Data is inseparable from models. It plays two critical roles in AI systems:

Training Data: The datasets on which models are trained fundamentally shape their capabilities, limitations, and behaviors. Engineers must understand what data a model was trained on, how this influences its outputs, and where the model’s knowledge gaps may lie.

Contextual Data: At inference time, models often require additional context beyond what is present in the immediate input. This may include:

  • Retrieved documents or facts from external sources
  • User history or session state
  • Task-specific instructions or examples
  • System constraints or configuration parameters

The engineering of data—both for training and inference—is a critical discipline. Data quality, relevance, and presentation format can dramatically affect system performance.

4.2.3 Prompt

The prompt layer serves as the interface between deterministic systems and probabilistic models. A prompt is not merely a string of text sent to a model—it is a structured specification that defines:

  • The task the model should perform
  • The context in which reasoning should occur
  • The format and constraints on the output
  • Examples that guide the model’s behavior

Effective prompt engineering is therefore a form of interface design. The prompt must be clear, comprehensive, and carefully structured to elicit the desired behavior from the model. Poorly designed prompts lead to ambiguous or incorrect outputs, while well-designed prompts enable reliable and predictable system behavior.

4.2.4 Tool

Tools extend the capabilities of models beyond their internal knowledge and reasoning. A tool is any deterministic system that a model can invoke to perform specific operations, including:

  • APIs: Web services that provide structured data or functionality
  • Databases: Systems for storing and retrieving information
  • Calculators: Computational utilities for mathematical operations
  • Code interpreters: Environments for executing generated code
  • Search engines: Systems for retrieving information from the web

The integration of tools enables models to access current information, perform precise calculations, and interact with external systems. However, tool integration introduces new engineering challenges: tools must have well-defined interfaces, predictable behavior, and appropriate safety mechanisms.

4.2.5 Agent

Agents introduce autonomy and decision-making into AI systems. While pipelines execute predefined sequences of operations, agents operate through iterative loops of observation, reasoning, and action. An agent is characterized by:

  • Observation: The ability to gather information about its environment
  • Reasoning: The capacity to interpret situations and determine appropriate actions
  • Action: The ability to execute operations, potentially including tool invocations
  • Planning: The capacity to decompose complex tasks into sequences of steps

Agents can pursue objectives through multiple iterations, adjusting their behavior based on results and feedback. This makes them suitable for tasks that require multi-step reasoning, exploration, or adaptation to changing conditions.

4.2.6 Pipeline

The pipeline layer structures the flow of information through an AI system. A pipeline is a sequence of stages, each performing a specific transformation on the data as it moves through the system. Typical pipeline stages include:

  • Input preprocessing and validation
  • Context retrieval from external sources
  • Prompt construction from templates and data
  • Model inference
  • Output processing and validation
  • Result formatting and delivery

Pipelines introduce structure and modularity into AI systems. They make information flow explicit, enable isolation of components, and support testing and debugging. Complex systems can be constructed by composing multiple pipelines into larger workflows.

4.2.7 Evaluation

The evaluation layer measures and assesses system behavior. Unlike traditional software testing, which verifies deterministic behavior against expected outputs, AI evaluation must assess probabilistic systems across distributions of inputs and conditions. Evaluation encompasses:

  • Accuracy: Whether outputs are correct or appropriate relative to defined tasks
  • Reliability: Whether the system performs consistently across varied inputs and conditions
  • Consistency: Whether outputs remain stable across similar or repeated inputs
  • Safety: Whether the system operates within acceptable boundaries and constraints

Evaluation frameworks provide the signals necessary to improve systems, detect regressions, and ensure reliability over time. They are essential for maintaining trustworthy AI systems.

4.2.8 Infrastructure

The infrastructure layer provides the operational foundation for AI systems. It includes:

  • Model serving: Systems for loading models into memory and exposing them through APIs
  • Vector databases: Specialized databases for storing and searching vector representations
  • Caching mechanisms: Systems for storing and reusing computed results to improve performance
  • Monitoring and observability: Tools for tracking system behavior, performance, and errors
  • Resource management: Systems for allocating and managing computational resources
  • Deployment and scaling: Mechanisms for deploying systems and handling varying loads

Infrastructure decisions have profound effects on system performance, cost, reliability, and scalability. Effective infrastructure design is essential for transitioning experimental prototypes into production services.

4.3 The Three Architectural Domains

While the eight layers provide a detailed view of AI system components, it is useful to group them into three broader architectural domains: the Model Layer, the Control Layer, and the System Layer.

4.3.1 Model Layer

Model Layer
    models
    data

The Model Layer forms the computational foundation of the system. It includes the trained models themselves and the data that shaped their behavior. At this level, intelligence is statistical rather than procedural. The model produces outputs by estimating probabilities across patterns learned from data.

The Model Layer provides raw intelligence, but this intelligence is undirected. A model alone cannot perform complex tasks without guidance, context, and structure. The remaining layers serve to harness and direct this intelligence toward specific goals.

4.3.2 Control Layer

Control Layer
    prompts
    tools
    agents

The Control Layer governs how models are used. It provides the mechanisms through which raw model intelligence is structured, directed, and applied to specific tasks.

  • Prompts act as structured interfaces, specifying the task, providing context, and constraining behavior
  • Tools extend capabilities, allowing models to interact with external systems and perform operations beyond their native abilities
  • Agents introduce autonomy, enabling systems to pursue objectives through iterative reasoning and action

Where the Model Layer provides intelligence, the Control Layer provides direction. The Control Layer transforms general model capabilities into specific, task-oriented behavior.

4.3.3 System Layer

System Layer
    pipelines
    evaluation
    infrastructure

The System Layer organizes the overall behavior of the application. It transforms individual components into functioning products that can serve users reliably at scale.

  • Pipelines structure information flow, organizing sequences of transformations that process inputs into outputs
  • Evaluation measures performance, providing feedback for improvement and ensuring reliability
  • Infrastructure provides the operational foundation, enabling deployment, monitoring, and scaling

The System Layer ensures that intelligence and control are embedded in systems that are usable, reliable, and maintainable.

4.4 Designing Across the Stack

The AI engineering stack is not merely a descriptive framework—it is a guide for system design. An effective AI engineer must understand and design across all three domains.

Focusing only on the Model Layer leads to research prototypes rather than deployable systems. A model with impressive capabilities remains isolated and unusable without the control mechanisms and system infrastructure to integrate it into applications.

Focusing only on the Control Layer leads to brittle systems that fail under real-world conditions. Clever prompts and tool integrations cannot compensate for lack of evaluation, observability, or robust infrastructure.

Focusing only on the System Layer leads to scalable but unintelligent pipelines. Sophisticated infrastructure cannot produce valuable outputs without models, prompts, and tools that provide actual intelligence.

Effective systems arise when intelligence, control, and system architecture are designed together. The engineer must consider:

  • Which models to use and how they will be integrated
  • How prompts will structure model behavior
  • Which tools will extend capabilities
  • Whether agents or pipelines better suit the task
  • How evaluation will ensure reliability
  • What infrastructure is required for deployment

These decisions are interconnected. The choice of model influences prompt design. The complexity of prompts affects evaluation requirements. The need for tools determines infrastructure demands. A change to any layer may require adjustments to others.

4.5 The Importance of Layered Thinking

The importance of this layered perspective cannot be overstated. In traditional software engineering, code itself defines the behavior of the system. In AI engineering, behavior emerges from the interaction of multiple layers.

Consider how different layers contribute to system behavior:

  • A prompt may change how a model reasons, significantly affecting output quality
  • A tool may expand the model’s capabilities, enabling tasks that would otherwise be impossible
  • A pipeline may restructure how information flows through the system, affecting efficiency and reliability
  • An evaluation mechanism may alter how the system evolves over time, driving continuous improvement
  • Infrastructure decisions affect latency, cost, and scalability, shaping the user experience

Thus the task of the AI engineer is not merely to select a model or craft a prompt, but to architect a stack in which each layer reinforces the others. The engineer must design at multiple levels simultaneously, ensuring that choices at one level are coherent with choices at others.

4.6 The Stack as a Conceptual Map

The AI engineering stack therefore provides both a conceptual map and a practical guide. It clarifies:

  • Where different technologies belong: New tools and frameworks can be placed within the stack based on their function
  • How responsibilities are distributed: Each layer has clear responsibilities, preventing overlap and confusion
  • How components interact: The stack defines relationships between layers, guiding integration and communication
  • Where to focus debugging efforts: When problems arise, the stack helps identify which layer may be responsible
  • How to scale development work: Different engineers or teams can focus on different layers while maintaining coherence

The stack also provides a shared vocabulary for discussing AI systems. Engineers can communicate precisely about which layer they are working on, what concerns they are addressing, and how their work relates to the overall system.

4.8 Conclusion

The AI engineering stack is more than a taxonomy of components. It is a framework for thinking about how intelligent systems are built and how they should be organized. By understanding the stack and its three architectural domains, engineers gain:

  • A systematic approach to system design
  • A shared vocabulary for communication
  • A guide for debugging and troubleshooting
  • A framework for scaling development work
  • A foundation for continuous learning and improvement

The stack reveals that AI engineering is not about mastering a single technology or technique. It is about orchestrating multiple layers—models, data, prompts, tools, agents, pipelines, evaluation, and infrastructure—into coherent systems that deliver reliable value to users.

This orchestration is the essence of AI engineering. It requires breadth of knowledge, depth of understanding, and the ability to think systematically across multiple levels of abstraction.

With this foundation established, we can now turn to the practical work of building AI systems. The next chapter explores the shift in thinking required—from functions to systems—that underpins all of AI engineering.
